01 Introduction

A Masterpiece of Memory and Martyrdom

Released in 1998 to coincide with the centennial of Philippine independence, José Rizal is a Philippine epic biographical drama directed by Marilou Diaz-Abaya and produced by GMA Films at a then-unprecedented budget of ₱125 million, making it the most expensive Filipino film at the time of its release. The film stars Cesar Montano in the titular role, with a supporting ensemble cast that includes Jaime Fabregas, Joel Torre, Gloria Diaz, Gina Alajar, Chin-Chin Gutierrez, Pen Medina, and Ronnie Lazaro. The screenplay was collaboratively written by three of the Philippines' most distinguished writers: Ricky Lee, Jun Lana, and Peter Ong Lim (Wikipedia, n.d.). The film swept the 1998 Metro Manila Film Festival with seventeen awards — the most wins for any single film in the festival's history — and was also selected for the Panorama program at the prestigious Berlin International Film Festival.

The purpose of this review is to critically examine José Rizal as both a biographical portrait and a historical document, assessing the film's fidelity to the historical record, its artistic and narrative choices, and its effectiveness as an educational and cultural artifact. This review also explores how the film connects to our class discussions on Rizal's life, works, and enduring legacy in the Philippine consciousness.

Thesis Statement

While José Rizal (1998) is a visually magnificent and historically grounded cinematic achievement that succeeds in humanizing the national hero, its ultimate significance lies in its deliberate blending of historical fact with literary imagination — most notably in its use of Rizal's fictional characters as interlocutors of his real life — a creative liberty that both complicates and deepens our understanding of how Rizal's pen became the sword that kindled a revolution, making the film an indispensable, if imperfect, educational lens through which Philippine identity and colonial history may be understood.

02 Summary of the Film

The Story Told Between Prison Walls

The film unfolds primarily through a series of non-linear flashbacks structured around Rizal's imprisonment in Fort Santiago in 1896, where he awaits trial and eventual execution by the Spanish colonial authorities. His court-appointed defense counsel, Lieutenant Luis Taviel de Andrade (Jaime Fabregas), listens to Rizal's life story in order to build a defense — and in so doing, becomes the film's audience surrogate, gradually transforming from a skeptic into a man deeply moved by the injustice of what he witnesses.

Through these flashbacks, the film traces Rizal's life from his childhood in Calamba, Laguna, under the intellectual nurture of his mother, Doña Teodora Alonzo (Gloria Diaz); his formative years at the Ateneo Municipal and the University of Santo Tomas; his departure for Europe to pursue advanced studies in medicine and to fight the colonial system through the pen; the writing and publication of Noli Me Tángere (1887) and El Filibusterismo (1891); his relationships with women, particularly Leonor Rivera; his exile to Dapitan; and finally, his arrest, sham trial, and execution by firing squad at Bagumbayan on December 30, 1896. Uniquely, the film interweaves scenes from Rizal's novels — with Joel Torre playing Crisóstomo Ibarra and Simoun, and Monique Wilson as María Clara — directly into the biographical narrative, using fictional characters to illuminate the lived realities of colonial oppression.

03 Historical Accuracy & Creative Liberties

Faithful to the Record, Bold in Its Imagination

Historical Accuracy

One of the film's most notable qualities is its commitment to historical research. The production team engaged in extensive archival work, and the result is evident in the authentic costumes, period-accurate dialogue, and meticulously recreated sets that transport viewers to the colonial Philippines of the late nineteenth century (Montebon, 2024; Letterboxd, n.d.). The major biographical facts of Rizal's life — his family background, his studies in Europe, the publication of his two landmark novels, his exile to Dapitan, and his execution — are represented with admirable fidelity.

Historically, José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, the seventh of eleven children born to Francisco Rizal Mercado and Teodora Alonzo (Britannica, n.d.; Wikipedia, 2024). Noli Me Tángere was indeed published in Berlin in 1887, and its sequel El Filibusterismo in Ghent in 1891 — both facts the film accurately portrays (University of Michigan, n.d.). Rizal's arrest following the outbreak of the Katipunan revolution in August 1896 — despite his having no direct involvement with Andrés Bonifacio's organization — and his subsequent sham trial and execution are portrayed in the film with historical accuracy consistent with primary sources and established scholarship (Britannica, n.d.; EBSCO, n.d.).

Historically Accurate Elements

  • Rizal's childhood and education in Calamba and Manila
  • Studies in Europe; publication of novels in Berlin and Ghent
  • Exile to Dapitan (1892–1896)
  • Arrest and imprisonment in Fort Santiago
  • Sham trial and execution by firing squad, December 30, 1896
  • Katipunan's uprising under Andrés Bonifacio
  • Period-accurate colonial social hierarchies and friar abuses

Creative Liberties Taken

  • Rizal conversing directly with his own fictional characters (Ibarra/Simoun)
  • Condensed and dramatized romantic relationships
  • Gardo Versoza's portrayal of Bonifacio as an idealized follower of Rizal
  • The framing device of Taviel as Rizal's intimate confidant
  • Dramatic compression of multi-year periods into single sequences

Evaluating the Creative Liberties

The film's most daring creative liberty — and arguably its most intellectually significant — is its insertion of Rizal's literary characters into the biographical narrative itself. Joel Torre's portrayal of both Crisóstomo Ibarra and the vengeful Simoun provides a remarkable cinematic device: by having the fictional Simoun confront the imprisoned Rizal and demand a different ending to El Filibusterismo, the film effectively poses the question of whether Rizal the reformist should have been Rizal the revolutionary (Montebon, 2024). This is a bold and philosophically rich liberty that enhances, rather than distorts, our understanding of the tension between peaceful reform and armed resistance in Rizal's thought.

"The film's greatest strength lies in its ability to present Rizal to his audience in the totality of his person. Through real and symbolic images, we identify with Rizal's aspirations, frustrations, fears and anger, as well as his failings." — Marivir R. Montebon, essayist and film commentator

However, not all creative choices are equally successful. The portrayal of Andrés Bonifacio by Gardo Versoza has been widely criticized as reducing the Supremo to a romantic figure of blind hero worship toward Rizal — a characterization that does a disservice to Bonifacio's documented independence of thought and his own intellectual and political agency (Oggsmoggs, 2007). This particular liberty potentially distorts the historical relationship between Rizal's reformism and Bonifacio's revolutionary nationalism, reinforcing a problematic hierarchy between the two figures.

The framing device of Taviel de Andrade also represents a creative compression. While Taviel was indeed Rizal's defense counsel (Britannica, n.d.), the intimate, confessional relationship depicted in the film goes beyond what can be historically verified. Nevertheless, this device serves a vital narrative function: it allows the audience to receive Rizal's story through sympathetic foreign eyes, thereby universalizing his significance beyond the boundaries of Philippine national identity.

04 Connection to Class Discussions

Rizal in the Classroom, Rizal on the Screen

Our class discussions on Rizal's life and works have consistently emphasized three central themes: the power of education and intellectual formation as instruments of national liberation; the tension between peaceful reform and armed revolution as competing paths to independence; and the enduring relevance of Rizal's critique of colonial power structures in the contemporary Philippine context. The 1998 film engages directly with all three of these themes.

The film's detailed depiction of Rizal's education — from informal lessons under his mother Doña Teodora to his studies at the Ateneo Municipal and eventual medical training in Europe — mirrors our class discussion of Rizal as a product of the ilustrado class who weaponized European Enlightenment ideals against colonial tyranny. His pen, as both the film and our discussions affirm, was a more dangerous instrument than any firearm, as evidenced by Noli Me Tángere's immediate banning by Spanish colonial authorities (EBSCO, n.d.).

The film also engages with the Propaganda Movement, which our discussions have identified as the intellectual precursor to the revolution. Rizal's contributions to La Solidaridad and the formation of the Liga Filipina in 1892 — both referenced in the film — represent his conviction, as we discussed in class, that enlightenment and legal reform were preferable to bloodshed. The tragic irony, brought to life in the film with remarkable emotional clarity, is that this peaceful convict became a martyr whose death did more to ignite the revolution than any political tract could have (Britannica, n.d.).

05 Reflection & Critical Analysis

Cinema as Historical Conscience

Personal Reflection - Jetrick Roxas

Watching José Rizal (1998) is not merely a cinematic experience — it is an encounter with conscience. What struck me most deeply was not the spectacle of colonial brutality nor the grandeur of the production design, but rather the film's insistence on presenting Rizal as a fully human figure: a man capable of love and longing, doubt and despair, not merely a bronze statue of civic virtue. Cesar Montano's performance, which I initially doubted could transcend his popular image, reveals itself as genuinely inhabited — his Rizal is brilliant yet vulnerable, principled yet achingly mortal.

The film profoundly altered my understanding of Rizal's martyrdom. Having studied his biography academically, I knew the facts of his execution. But the film's rendering of his final hours — his calm, his writing of Mi Último Adiós, his compassion even for his accusers — transforms historical knowledge into emotional understanding. It is one thing to know that Rizal died for his country; it is another to feel the weight of that sacrifice.

Film's Contemporary Relevance

The film effectively argues, through its narrative structure, that Rizal's significance is not reducible to his martyrdom but extends to his model of engaged, principled, and intellectual citizenship. In a contemporary Philippines still grappling with questions of press freedom, judicial independence, and the rights of minorities against entrenched power structures, the film's portrait of a man persecuted for his writings remains uncomfortably timely. As Rizal wrote in La Solidaridad and his novels, the evils of colonial society — corruption, complicity, the weaponization of religion against the powerless — are not merely historical curiosities but living patterns (Encyclopedia.com, n.d.).

Cinematography and Direction

Cinematographer Rody Lacap, one of the Philippines' most distinguished directors of photography — known for his work on Mike de Leon's Kisapmata and Peque Gallaga's Oro, Plata, Mata — brings a stark, painterly beauty to the film's visual language (Oggsmoggs, 2007). The interiors of Fort Santiago are rendered in shadow and candlelight, evoking both the literal imprisonment of Rizal's body and the metaphorical darkness of colonial rule. The flashback sequences, by contrast, employ warmer, more luminous palettes, particularly in the European scenes where Rizal's intellectual world opens up.

The score by Nonong Buencamino — which won a MMFF award — achieves a rare emotional register: mournful but never melodramatic, its motifs threading through the film like a recurring lament (IMDB, n.d.). Director Diaz-Abaya, who later received the Order of National Artists posthumously in 2022, brings to the film the feminist and humanist sensibility evident in her earlier works (Brutal, Moral, Karnal), insisting throughout on the humanity of all who suffer under colonial power — women, laborers, and intellectuals alike (Montebon, 2024).

Weaknesses and Limitations

The film is not without its flaws. The script, despite its literary pedigree, has been criticized for lacking tight dramatic structure — certain conversations that promise intellectual depth are abandoned before reaching their conclusions (IMDB, n.d.). The portrayal of several supporting historical figures — most notably Andrés Bonifacio — suffers from oversimplification, reducing complex historical actors to symbolic functions within Rizal's story. This reflects a broader tendency in nationalist historiography: the elevation of Rizal's narrative necessarily casts shadows over other equally significant figures and trajectories. The film is, ultimately, as one critic observed, "basically a history book adapted to film" — a quality that serves educational purposes but occasionally constrains its artistic ambitions (Oggsmoggs, 2007).

"Jose Rizal may not be the best film that the Philippines has to offer. But it was a stepping stone in excellence for the local film industry." — IMDB Reviewer

Beyond the Hero: Seeing Rizal as Human

Personal Reflection - Josh Laparan

Realizing and observing Jose Rizal gave me a different kind of realization—it made me question how I have always viewed heroes in history. Instead of presenting Rizal as someone distant and perfect, the film allowed me to see him as a person who struggled with emotions, relationships, and uncertainty. This perspective made his story feel more real to me, because it showed that greatness does not come from being flawless, but from choosing to stand firm despite fear and doubt.

I also found myself appreciating the performance of Cesar Montano more than I expected. His portrayal did not feel exaggerated or overly dramatic; instead, it felt grounded and sincere. He captured Rizal not just as an intellectual figure, but as someone who carried the weight of his decisions. Through his acting, I could sense the internal conflict Rizal experienced, which made the story more engaging and emotionally powerful.

What impacted me the most was how the film presented Rizal’s final moments. Reading about his execution in the past felt distant and factual, but seeing it unfold visually made it heavier and more personal. Watching him face death with calmness while finishing Mi Último Adiós made me realize the depth of his courage. It showed me that his sacrifice was not just an event in history, but a conscious and meaningful choice.

In the end, the film reminded me that Rizal’s legacy is not only about what he achieved, but also about how he lived and what he stood for. It challenged me to think about my own values and whether I would have the same strength to stand for what is right, even in the face of fear.

“It is a useless life that is not consecrated to a great ideal.” — José Rizal

Watching José Rizal (1998) made me realize that Rizal’s heroism was not rooted in perfection, but in his willingness to dedicate his life to a purpose greater than himself. The film revealed his humanity—his fears, struggles, and sacrifices—which made his ideals even more powerful. It reminded me that true courage is not the absence of doubt, but the decision to stand firm despite it.

Personal Reflection - Jonard Vieney Ybay

For me, this film frames Rizal’s struggle in a surprisingly relatable way. He wasn't just writing novels; he was essentially trying to refactor a deeply corrupted social system from the ground up. Instead of pushing for a brute-force approach that might crash the whole structure, he focused on long-term optimization through education and clear communication. Watching how he navigated conflicting ideologies among his peers highlighted the real-world importance of effective conflict management and adaptive leadership. It serves as a stark reminder that true leadership requires analyzing systemic flaws, understanding complex organizational dynamics, and carefully architecting solutions that empower people, rather than just forcing a chaotic disruption. Ultimately, Rizal’s legacy challenges me to view my education not merely as a way to build applications or advance a technical career, but as a structured framework to thoughtfully debug the injustices in the world around me.

Personal Reflection - Samantha Sabangan

The film Jose Rizal (1998), directed by Marilou Diaz-Abaya, presents more than a retelling of history. It explores the mind and identity of José Rizal, showing how his ideas were shaped by the injustices around him. Its message centers on the power of knowledge, critical thinking, and nationalism as tools for change. Instead of glorifying revolution through violence, the film highlights how Rizal used his writings to challenge oppression and awaken national consciousness.

What made the film meaningful to me was how it humanized Rizal. He was not shown as a perfect hero, but as someone who questioned himself and struggled with the consequences of his beliefs. This made his decisions feel more real and impactful. The blending of his life with scenes from Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo helped me see that his works were deeply connected to reality, serving as reflections of society rather than mere fiction.

The film effectively conveys Rizal’s importance in Philippine history by showing how his ideas influenced others even after his death. His execution was portrayed not just as an ending, but as a turning point that inspired further resistance. This made me realize that his contributions continue to be relevant, especially in understanding issues of identity, freedom, and social responsibility today.

In terms of filmmaking, the direction and cinematography created a serious and reflective atmosphere. The transitions between different timelines and the use of symbolic imagery emphasized Rizal’s inner thoughts and development. The performance of Cesar Montano added emotional depth, making Rizal more relatable and grounded. These elements enhanced the film’s effectiveness as an educational tool by making complex ideas easier to understand and engage with.

At the same time, the film includes creative interpretations that may not fully align with historical records. This highlights the need to view films as interpretations rather than exact representations of history. It encouraged me to think more critically and to compare what I see on screen with reliable sources.

Overall, the film succeeds in presenting Rizal’s life and ideas in a compelling and thought-provoking way. It deepened my appreciation of his role in shaping the nation and made me reflect on how his values can still apply today. As an educational film, it is effective not only in informing viewers but also in encouraging reflection and critical thinking about history and society.

Watching Jose Rizal (1998) felt deeper than I expected. At first, I thought it would just be another historical film to understand for school, but it ended up making me think for myself. What really stood out was how Rizal used his skills as his weapon. He did not rely on force. He relied on his mind, his creativity, and his courage. That made me realize that the things I spend time on, like coding, music, or even gaming, are not just hobbies or skills for personal gain, but something that can be used for a bigger purpose.

It reminded me that even people who achieve great things do not always have everything figured out. They just keep moving forward. That hit me, especially during times when I feel tired or unsure about what I am doing.

I also realized how powerful this film is for many people. After watching and seeing how others reacted to it, it is clear that it is not just a requirement but something that truly connects with Filipinos. It made me appreciate not just the actors, but also the people behind the scenes who helped shape the film into something meaningful.

One of the biggest realizations I had was about nationalism. It used to feel like a big idea, but the film made it more personal. It is not always about grand actions, it can be about using what you have to contribute in your own way. In the end, it was tiring to process because it made me think deeply, but it was worth it. 06 Conclusion

A Monument in Celluloid

José Rizal (1998) stands as one of the most significant achievements in Philippine cinema — a film that is simultaneously a historical document, an artistic endeavor, and a national act of remembrance. Directed with intelligence and feeling by Marilou Diaz-Abaya, anchored by Cesar Montano's career-defining performance, and illuminated by Rody Lacap's cinematography, it succeeds in presenting José Rizal not as a marble monument but as a breathing, striving, suffering human being whose choices continue to resonate in the Philippine present.

Its historical accuracy, while largely commendable, is complicated by creative liberties that range from the illuminating (the Ibarra/Simoun dialogues with Rizal) to the reductive (the portrayal of Bonifacio). Nevertheless, even its most questionable liberties serve the film's larger purpose: to make Rizal's life not merely comprehensible but urgent, not merely past but present. The film is strongly recommended to anyone seeking a cinematic entry point into the life of the Philippines' most complex and consequential historical figure.

4
out of 5
Very Satisfactory

The film earns a 4 out of 5 for its exceptional production values, historically grounded portrayal of Rizal's life, and cultural significance — tempered by structural weaknesses in the screenplay and occasional reductive characterizations of secondary historical figures. It is essential viewing for any student of Philippine history and identity.

Ultimately, José Rizal (1998) achieves something rare in national biography on film: it makes us feel the cost of conscience. In doing so, it fulfills Rizal's own lifelong project — to awaken in his countrymen the understanding that freedom is not a gift granted by the powerful but a right wrested through sacrifice, intelligence, and the unbreakable conviction that one's people deserve better. That message, over a century after his execution, remains as vital as ever.
